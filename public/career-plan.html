The good news: the dream is genuinely realistic for him

Most people chasing "remote for a Swiss company, Swiss salary" hit a wall of legal/tax problems — permanent-establishment risk, social-security gaps, work-permit issues. None of that applies to him. Because he lives in Switzerland and is a Swiss national:

  • He can be hired as a normal Swiss employee — no Employer of Record, no cross-border structuring, no permit needed.
  • Remote and hybrid work for Swiss companies is completely standard post-2020. Swiss employers happily let Swiss-resident staff work from home.
  • He's already inside the highest-paying tech labour market in Europe, with the right to work in it.
So the dream isn't the hard part. The only real challenge is becoming a hireable engineer. That's a pure skill-and-evidence problem — exactly what this plan solves.

What the money looks like (2026 data)

  • Entry-level / junior software engineer in Switzerland: ~CHF 65k–105k (Zurich at the top end).
  • Median Swiss developer salary: ~CHF 100k–110k+, among the highest in Europe; senior/specialist roles go well above CHF 130k.
  • Most Swiss tech roles are now hybrid by default, and fully-remote Swiss roles are common and growing — so "remote for a Swiss company" is a normal job posting for someone resident in CH.

The path to financial freedom here is straightforward in shape: land a Swiss junior dev role → grow to mid/senior over a few years → bank the (large) gap between a Swiss tech salary and living costs.

His built-in advantages (use them)

  1. Residency + nationality — no visa, no relocation, full access to Swiss employers and Swiss salaries.
  2. Language — as a Swiss national he likely already has German, French, or Italian plus English. That is a major hiring multiplier; many international candidates can't compete on local-language roles. Keep English at professional level; it's the working language of most Swiss tech teams.
  3. Local network & on-site option — he can do internships, meetups, and on-site interviews easily, then negotiate remote/hybrid once he's proven.

Guiding principles

  1. Each phase should be self-funding or self-justifying. Land small paid work early so motivation and money both stay alive.
  2. Build in public. A public GitHub + a few shipped projects beats a certificate. Swiss employers hire evidence.
  3. Depth beats breadth. Real CS fundamentals are what separate a CHF 70k coder from a CHF 120k engineer.
  4. Ship > study. Always have one project in flight that uses what's being learned.
  5. Leverage the local angle. On-site/hybrid first if needed to break in, then convert to remote — being physically in CH makes that easy.

The 8-phase pathway

A realistic full-time pace is ~2.5–3 years to a solid remote Swiss role; part-time (while keeping a current job) is ~3.5–4 years. Adjust the calendar, keep the order.

Phase 0 — Foundations & honest self-assessment (Month 0–1)

Goal: decide it's real, set up the environment, remove excuses.

  • Pick a primary language to learn programming with: Python (gentlest, most versatile) or JavaScript (if web is the immediate interest).
  • Set up the toolkit: a laptop that runs VS Code, Git, and a terminal. Create a GitHub account today.
  • Decide a weekly time budget and protect it in a calendar (even 8–10 focused hrs/week compounds).
  • Quick wins: freeCodeCamp's first certificate, or the first weeks of Harvard CS50x (free, world-class on-ramp).
Milestone: a public GitHub repo with a tiny program he wrote himself, and a fixed weekly study schedule.

Phase 1 — Programming fundamentals (Month 1–4)

Goal: write real programs and think in code.

  • CS50x (Harvard, free on edX) — C, Python, SQL, web basics, and how computers actually work. Finish it; it's the backbone.
  • Reinforce with daily small problems (Exercism, or Codewars in moderation).
  • Learn Git/GitHub properly — branches, commits, pull requests. A job skill, not optional.
Milestone: CS50 final project shipped to GitHub; comfortable writing a 150–300 line program from a blank file.

Phase 2 — Pick a track & get job-ready (Month 4–10)

Goal: become hireable in one concrete specialization.

Choose ONE track to go deep (he can broaden later):

  • Web (recommended starter — most jobs, most remote-friendly): HTML/CSS → JavaScript → React (frontend) and/or Node.js + a database (backend). The roadmap.sh roadmaps are the map.
  • Data / Python: SQL, pandas, APIs, a bit of cloud → data-analyst then data-engineer roles (strong demand in Swiss finance/pharma).
  • Cloud / DevOps: Linux, networking, Docker, one cloud (AWS/Azure) + an entry cert — very well paid in CH.

Build 3–4 portfolio projects that solve a believable problem (not todo-app #9). One should be deployed live with a URL.

Milestone: a portfolio site + 3 deployed projects + a CV listing them.

Phase 3 — First income (Month 8–14, overlaps Phase 2)

Goal: get money flowing in — this funds and validates everything.

  • Freelance micro-work: small sites, bug fixes, automations for local Swiss businesses (huge advantage — local language + local presence).
  • Open-source contributions → credibility.
  • Internships / apprenticeships / junior roles, including Swiss apprenticeship-style routes and entry roles at Swiss SMEs.

Even a few hundred CHF/month here is a psychological turning point: it proves the skills convert to money and keeps the plan funded.

Milestone: first real paid project completed; one client testimonial.

Phase 4 — Computer-science depth (Month 10–24, runs in parallel)

Goal: stop being a "tutorial coder," become an engineer. This is the salary unlock.

  • Teach Yourself CS — the 9 core subjects, by ROI: programming fundamentals → data structures & algorithms → computer architecture → databases & data-intensive apps → networking, OS, languages/compilers, distributed systems, math as needed.
  • "Degree-like" alternative: OSSU Computer Science (free, ~2 years at 20 hrs/week).
  • Grind algorithm problems (LeetCode patterns) only when job hunting, not endlessly.
Milestone: can explain time complexity, design a database schema, and pass a junior technical interview.

Phase 5 — First salaried Swiss developer role (Month 18–30)

Goal: a full-time junior developer job at a Swiss company.

  • Job boards: swissdevjobs.ch (stacks + salaries), jobs.ch, LinkedIn (Switzerland filter), WeAreDevelopers, plus company career pages.
  • Many Swiss roles are hybrid — perfectly fine as a first job; it gets his foot in the door and remote can follow.
  • Optimize the LinkedIn + GitHub + portfolio trio; Swiss recruiters search these heavily.
  • Use the local-language advantage for roles international candidates can't take.
Milestone: signed offer for a salaried developer role at a Swiss company. Income now covers life + savings → the first real taste of financial freedom.

Phase 6 — Specialize, raise comp, build leverage (Year 2–3)

Goal: go from "a junior" to "the person you want on the team."

  • Deepen into a high-value niche: cloud/platform, data engineering, backend at scale, security, or AI/ML engineering — all pay premiums in CH and are remote-friendly.
  • Earn 1–2 respected certifications if the niche rewards them.
  • Switch jobs every ~18–24 months early on if it meaningfully raises comp — the fastest salary-growth lever in the first few years, and Swiss pay scales high.
  • Keep shipping visible work; start writing/speaking a little to build a name.
Milestone: mid-level engineer title and a salary comfortably above living costs, with savings/investing in place.

Phase 7 — Lock in the remote Swiss role (Year 2–3+)

Goal: convert proven experience into the original dream — remote (or remote-heavy) work for a Swiss company at a Swiss salary.

  • Negotiate remote/hybrid in his current role once he's a trusted performer (easiest path — most Swiss employers say yes to proven staff).
  • Target remote-first Swiss companies directly when job-switching — common now, and they pay full Swiss salaries to Swiss residents.
  • Optionally combine with cost-of-living flexibility within CH (live in a cheaper canton, work remote for a Zurich/Geneva employer) to boost real savings.
Milestone: working remotely (or his preferred hybrid split) for a Swiss company at a Swiss salary — the dream, now real because the skills, evidence, and leverage are all in place.

Financial-freedom layer (runs the whole time)

A Swiss tech salary is one of the strongest wealth engines in the world if the spending gap is banked:

  • Bank the income gap. As pay rises (Swiss raises are large), keep lifestyle flat for a while and save/invest the difference.
  • Use the Swiss pillar system: max out Pillar 3a (tax-advantaged retirement) and understand Pillar 2 — major, often-overlooked wealth tools for Swiss earners. (Worth a session with a fee-only adviser.)
  • Build a 6-month emergency fund before any risk (job changes).
  • Invest the surplus (low-cost global index funds) rather than letting CHF sit idle.
  • Optional side income: freelance on top of a salary once skills are strong — genuinely realistic in tech and accelerates freedom.
  • Cost-of-living arbitrage within CH: a remote Swiss salary while living in a cheaper canton is one of the fastest legitimate routes to financial independence available anywhere.

A note on mindset

The single biggest predictor of success isn't talent — it's showing up weekly for years and shipping visible work. The plan is deliberately front-loaded with small wins (first program, first deployed project, first paid gig) so motivation and money arrive early and keep the engine running. And because he's already a Swiss resident national, the finish line — a remote Swiss job at a Swiss salary — is not a long shot. It's the natural outcome of becoming a good engineer where he already lives.
