The shape of it

You're not starting from zero — you're a CS graduate with the ability to earn. The problem isn't capability, it's that money stress and disorganisation are eating the headspace you need to make good moves. So the order matters:

  1. Stabilise the money first — so the background panic drops and you can think.
  2. Keep freelance income flowing — it funds the search and proves your skills convert to cash.
  3. Run a real job search — as a system, not a mood.
  4. Sharpen skills as evidence — shipped work beats another certificate.
  5. Then grow income and start building wealth — once the base is solid.
The hardest part is already behind you (the degree, the skills). This plan is about systems and sequence, not heroics — small consistent moves, tracked in your Life Hub.

Guiding principles

  1. Stabilise before you optimise. Don't chase the perfect role while a fine is escalating.
  2. Two engines running: freelance income now, permanent search in parallel — don't pause one for the other.
  3. Evidence over credentials. A live project + clean GitHub beats another course.
  4. Systems over willpower. The weekly review and the Hub carry the load so motivation doesn't have to.
  5. Bank the gap. When income rises, keep spending flat for a while and save the difference.

Phase 1 — Stop the bleeding (now → ~2 weeks)

Goal: see all of it, stop anything escalating, drop the background panic.

  • In the Life Hub: capture every parking fine with its pay-by date, and every bill/debt. Pay or contest the soonest fines before they escalate.
  • Set up the weekly 15-minute money review and do the first one.
  • Cancel or pause non-essential spend while you catch up.
  • Open the post; deal with anything official. If you're freelancing, make sure you're registered for HMRC Self Assessment and setting tax aside.
Milestone: every fine and bill captured with a deadline — nothing unknown, nothing escalating by surprise.

Phase 2 — Stabilise cash flow (week 2–8)

Goal: a predictable monthly income floor and the bleeding fully stopped.

  • Build a steady freelance pipeline: define your offer and rates, and know where the next client comes from — referrals, your network, local businesses, agencies/contract platforms, and proof-of-work sites (Upwork / PeoplePerHour) if useful. Aim for a predictable monthly minimum.
  • Know your number: the essential monthly cost of your life. Keep lifestyle flat while you rebuild.
  • Clear arrears, and arrange smaller payment plans where a month is tight (free help below — contact creditors early, don't go silent).
  • Set aside ~25–30% of freelance income for tax in a separate pot. If contracting via a limited company or "inside IR35," understand what that means for take-home.
  • Start a tiny buffer — even £20–50/week — so the next surprise isn't a crisis.
Milestone: a predictable freelance baseline + a starter buffer + nothing overdue.

Phase 3 — Land the role (week 4–16, overlaps Phase 2)

Goal: an active pipeline of applications → interviews → an offer for a strong role.

  • Treat the search as a system, tracked in the Hub: applications out, responses, interview stages.
  • Sharpen the trio: a results-focused CV, a strong LinkedIn, and a tidy GitHub/portfolio. Recruiters search all three.
  • Target London & UK-remote software roles (junior/mid). Boards: LinkedIn, Otta / Welcome to the Jungle, cord, Hired, Indeed, plus company career pages and dev recruiters.
  • Cadence: a steady weekly number of quality applications (e.g. 5–10) plus a little networking, beats occasional bursts.
  • Interview prep, when actively interviewing: DS&A patterns (LeetCode), system-design basics for mid roles, and a few solid STAR behavioural stories.
  • Use your freelance work as portfolio evidence — real clients, real shipped things.
Milestone: an active interview pipeline, and ideally a signed offer for a role that moves you forward.

Phase 4 — Skills & portfolio (ongoing, in parallel)

Goal: be visibly good at one thing, with shipped work to prove it.

  • Pick a focus — full-stack web, backend, data, or cloud/DevOps. Depth beats breadth for getting hired and paid.
  • Ship 1–2 real projects with live URLs and clean READMEs; contribute to open source for credibility.
  • Keep GitHub active and write up what you build — it compounds into a reputation.
Milestone: 1–2 deployed portfolio projects and a clear "this is what I'm good at" story.

Phase 5 — Grow income & build wealth (month 4–12+)

Goal: rising income and the first real foundations of financial stability.

  • Negotiate rates/salary, and don't be afraid to switch roles for a meaningful jump — early-career moves are the fastest pay-growth lever.
  • Build an emergency fund to 3–6 months of essential costs before taking risks.
  • UK wealth basics: use a Stocks & Shares ISA (tax-free growth), capture any workplace-pension employer match, favour low-cost global index funds, and avoid lifestyle creep as pay rises. (MoneyHelper is a good free, gov-backed starting point.)
  • Optional: keep a little freelance income on top of a salary to accelerate savings.
Milestone: emergency fund in place, investing started, and income trending up — financial stability becoming real.

Resources (UK)

Jobs

  • Boards: LinkedIn (London filter), Otta / Welcome to the Jungle, cord, Hired, Indeed, plus company career pages.
  • Interview prep: LeetCode (patterns), the "System Design Primer" (free on GitHub), STAR stories for behavioural rounds.

Money & debt (free, regulated)

  • National Debtline — 0808 808 4000 · StepChange · Citizens Advice. Free, confidential, can set up one plan across all debts.
  • MoneyHelper (gov-backed) — budgeting, ISAs, pensions, emergency fund basics.

Freelance / tax

  • HMRC Self Assessment — register, file, and set tax aside as you earn; understand IR35 if contracting.

Skills

  • roadmap.sh, freeCodeCamp, Exercism, and shipping real projects.

A note on mindset

The background money stress is the thing distorting everything right now — that's why Phase 1 is just "see it and stop it escalating." Once that panic drops, you're a capable CS graduate with two working income routes and a clear path. Show up weekly, keep both engines running, and let the systems carry the consistency. The finish line — stable money and a role you actually want — is a sequence of small moves from where you already are.

This is a personal planning document, not financial or legal advice. For debt you can't pay, talk to a free regulated service (National Debtline, StepChange, Citizens Advice).
